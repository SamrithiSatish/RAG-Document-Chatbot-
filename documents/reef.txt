The Great Barrier Reef is the world's largest coral reef system, located in the Coral Sea off the coast of Queensland, Australia. It stretches over 2,300 kilometers and is composed of over 2,900 individual reefs and 900 islands. The reef supports an extraordinary diversity of life, including 1,500 species of fish, 400 species of coral, and 4,000 species of mollusk.

The reef was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1981 due to its outstanding biodiversity and ecological significance. Despite its protected status, the reef faces significant threats from climate change, particularly rising ocean temperatures which cause coral bleaching. Major bleaching events occurred in 2016, 2017, and 2020, affecting large sections of the reef.

Coral bleaching happens when corals expel the symbiotic algae living in their tissues due to stress, usually caused by changes in temperature, light, or nutrients. Without these algae, corals turn white and lose their primary food source. If the stress continues, the coral will eventually die, though bleached coral can recover if conditions improve.

Conservation efforts for the Great Barrier Reef include the Reef 2050 Long-Term Sustainability Plan, which aims to improve water quality, restore coral habitats, and address the impacts of climate change. Scientists are also researching heat-resistant coral strains and exploring methods to assist coral reproduction and reef restoration.

Tourism plays a major economic role in the region, generating billions of dollars annually and supporting tens of thousands of jobs. However, tourism itself must be carefully managed to minimize its environmental impact on the reef ecosystem.
