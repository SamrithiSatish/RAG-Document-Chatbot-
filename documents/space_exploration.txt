The Apollo 11 mission was the first crewed mission to land humans on the Moon. It launched on July 16, 1969, and Neil Armstrong became the first person to walk on the lunar surface on July 20, 1969, followed shortly after by Buzz Aldrin. The mission's command module pilot, Michael Collins, remained in lunar orbit during the moonwalk.

The Apollo program was managed by NASA and was part of a broader effort during the Space Race with the Soviet Union. The program aimed to fulfill President John F. Kennedy's goal, announced in 1961, of landing a man on the Moon before the end of the decade.

Apollo 11's lunar module, named Eagle, landed in the Sea of Tranquility. Armstrong's famous words upon stepping onto the surface were "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." The astronauts spent about two and a half hours outside the spacecraft, collecting samples and conducting experiments.

The mission returned to Earth on July 24, 1969, splashing down in the Pacific Ocean. In total, the Apollo program conducted six successful crewed Moon landings between 1969 and 1972, with Apollo 17 being the final mission. Twelve astronauts in total walked on the Moon during this period.

The Apollo missions provided significant scientific returns, including hundreds of kilograms of lunar rock and soil samples that helped scientists understand the Moon's composition and origin. The program also drove major advances in computing, materials science, and telecommunications that had lasting effects beyond space exploration.
